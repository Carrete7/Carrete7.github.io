What is Power Factor (PF)?

In alternating current (AC) electrical systems, the Power Factor is the ratio of Real Power (kW) that actually performs useful work (like generating heat, light, or motion) to the Apparent Power (kVA) that the utility company must supply to the circuit.

Industrial loads such as induction motors, transformers, and fluorescent lighting ballasts require magnetic fields to operate. Creating these fields consumes Reactive Power (kVAR). This reactive power "bounces" back and forth between the generator and the load without doing any real work, but it still takes up space on the electrical grid.

The Beer Mug Analogy

A classic way to understand this is the "Beer Mug Analogy":

  • The Liquid Beer (kW): This is the Real Power. It's what you actually want and pay for to quench your thirst (do work).
  • The Foam (kVAR): This is the Reactive Power. It takes up space in the glass but doesn't quench your thirst.
  • The Whole Mug (kVA): This is the Apparent Power. It's the total capacity the bartender (the utility company) has to deliver to give you your beer.

How to Calculate the Required kVAR

To fix a poor power factor (usually anything below 0.90), engineers install a Capacitor Bank. Capacitors act as local generators of reactive power, supplying the "foam" directly to the motors so the utility company doesn't have to push it all the way down the power lines.

The mathematical formula to size the capacitor bank in kilovolt-amperes reactive (kVAR) is based on trigonometry (The Power Triangle):

kVAR = kW × ( tan(cos-1(PF1)) - tan(cos-1(PF2)) )

Where:

  • kW = Total Real Power load of the facility.
  • PF1 = The existing (initial) Power Factor before correction.
  • PF2 = The target (desired) Power Factor after correction (typically 0.95 or 0.98).

Benefits of Power Factor Correction

Installing a properly sized capacitor bank offers immediate financial and technical returns on investment (ROI):

  1. Elimination of Utility Penalties: Most power companies charge hefty fines if your PF drops below 0.90 or 0.95. Correction eliminates these monthly fees.
  2. Increased System Capacity: By reducing the reactive power (foam), you free up space in your transformers and cables (kVA), allowing you to add more machines without upgrading your main electrical service.
  3. Reduced Line Losses: Lower total current (Amps) means less heat generated in the cables, improving overall efficiency and reducing voltage drop.
