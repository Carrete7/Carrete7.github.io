Wire Sizing & Voltage Drop Explained

7 min read Electrical Design

Sizing electrical conductors correctly is not just a matter of functionality; it is a matter of strict safety and fire prevention. If a wire is too small for the load it carries, its internal resistance will cause it to overheat, potentially melting the insulation and causing an electrical fire.

Furthermore, running undersized cables over long distances results in excessive Voltage Drop. When voltage drops, motors run hot, lights dim, and sensitive electronics fail. In this guide, we will walk you through the professional method of sizing wires in American Wire Gauge (AWG) based on the National Electrical Code (NEC).

Step 1: Determine the Load (Amperage)

Before selecting a wire, you must know how much current (Amps) the circuit will draw. If you only know the wattage of your equipment, you can use our Ohm's Law & Power Calculator to convert Watts to Amps.

If you are sizing a circuit for an industrial motor, the process is slightly different because you must account for the motor's power factor and efficiency. We recommend using our 3-Phase Motor Power Calculator to find the exact Full Load Amperage (FLA) before proceeding.

Step 2: Understand NEC Voltage Drop Limits

According to NEC Article 210.19(A) Informational Note No. 4, conductors for branch circuits should be sized to prevent a voltage drop exceeding 3% at the farthest outlet. For a combination of feeder and branch circuits, the total drop should not exceed 5%.

  • Example on a 120V circuit: A 3% maximum drop means you cannot lose more than $120 \times 0.03 = \mathbf{3.6 \text{ Volts}}$.
  • Example on a 480V circuit: A 3% maximum drop means you cannot lose more than $480 \times 0.03 = \mathbf{14.4 \text{ Volts}}$.

Step 3: The Circular Mils (CM) Formula

To find the physical cross-sectional area of the wire needed to meet the NEC voltage drop requirement, electrical engineers calculate the Circular Mils (CM). The formula depends on whether the system is Single-Phase or Three-Phase.

Single-Phase (1-Phase):
$$ CM = \frac{2 \times K \times I \times D}{V_{drop}} $$
Three-Phase (3-Phase):
$$ CM = \frac{1.732 \times K \times I \times D}{V_{drop}} $$

What are these variables?

  • $CM$ = Wire area required in Circular Mils.
  • $K$ = Direct Current Resistance constant. This is 12.9 for Copper and 21.2 for Aluminum.
  • $I$ = Current load in Amperes.
  • $D$ = One-way length of the wire in Feet.
  • $V_{drop}$ = The maximum allowable voltage drop you calculated in Step 2.

Pro Tip: If you are feeding power from a step-down transformer, calculate the primary and secondary voltage drops separately. You can find primary and secondary amperage using our Transformer FLA Calculator.

Step 4: Convert Circular Mils to AWG

Once you calculate the required Circular Mils, you must look at a standard AWG table to find the commercial wire size that matches or exceeds your CM value. For example, if your calculation results in 14,000 CM, a 10 AWG wire (10,380 CM) is too small, so you must jump up to an 8 AWG wire (16,510 CM).

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Why is Aluminum wire thicker than Copper for the same Amps?

Aluminum has a higher electrical resistance (a K-factor of 21.2) compared to Copper (K-factor of 12.9). Because it resists the flow of electricity more, an aluminum conductor must have a larger physical diameter (higher AWG/kcmil) to carry the same amount of current without overheating.

What is kcmil (MCM)?

The AWG (American Wire Gauge) system only goes up to size 4/0 (0000). For massive industrial cables carrying hundreds of amps, the industry switches to kcmil (kilo-circular mils), previously known as MCM. For example, a 250 kcmil cable has a cross-sectional area of exactly 250,000 Circular Mils.
